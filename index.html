Voter Suppression or Voter Fraud? The Demographics Underlying the Presidential Election Battle

By Charlene Freeman, NYCity News Service

Mon November 23, 2020

A protester displays a sign in front of the CNN center Wednesday, Nov. 4, 2020 in Atlanta. (AP Photo/John Bazemore)

Pennsylvania’s Supreme Court on Monday rejected President Donald Trump’s attempt to block the counting of 10,000 absentee ballots in the state, including in Philadelphia. This marks another defeat in the Trump campaign’s efforts around the country to prove voter fraud and overturn Joe Biden’s presidential win.

Michigan Board of Canvassers also voted to certify its election results on Monday, confirming Biden’s victory there. It follows the Trump team’s failure to stop certification in Wayne County, MI - a Democratic-leaning county that includes the city of Detroit, which has an African-American majority. A group of Michigan voters are now suing Trump in D.C. federal court for mass voter suppression.

These legal battles are taking place in key states that have urban centers like Detroit and Philadelphia, which have a majority or large black electorate. Underlying this is also a long-trending demographic reality – a majority of voters in all major racial/ethnic groups in the U.S. identify as Democratic (or Democratic-leaning) voters, except whites. This underlies why the Republican Party uses voter fraud allegations to combat increasing electoral participation of people of color and racially provocative campaign tactics to motivate its largely white base. These GOP tactics precede Trump, who has accelerated them.
